Sixteenth-Century Ambassadors*

Since the 1980s and 1990s, the idea of a European state system during the early modern period has become increasingly difficult to discuss. Whereas previous historians such as M.S. Anderson and Garrett Mattingly could describe a European system constrained to the limits of the major powers of western Europe, recent historiographical developments have made it increasingly difficult to continue such a narrative.1 No longer do we consider permenant ambassadors the sole preserve reflecting relationships between states, and we are recognizing the importance of relationships with states beyond western Europe that informed French, English, Spanish, and others' foreign policy decisions. More evidence each year is being published describing diplomatic relationships that transcended these limited bounds. Informal intermediaries played essential roles alongside, or even in lieu of, official ambassadors. Almost all the states of Europe had significant diplomatic and political relationships with the Ottoman Empire, and many of them sought out alliances with the Safavid Empire in Persia.2Because of this research we have a much greater understanding of how, through whom, and which countries states maintained ongoing political contacts. Nevertheless, we have yet to include this new research into a systematic evaluation of Europe's geo-political environment in the early modern period. Indeed, such a project has yet to be produced for France.

The sixteenth century is an especially

At the beginning of the sixteenth century, French diplomacy dramatically increased. This development is observable in the number of permanent embassies maintained across Europe. In 1516, One year after Francis I (r. 1515-1547) ascended to the throne, France only had two residential ambassadors abroad, located at the courts of England and Venice. When François Ier died in 1547, France maintained at least ten ambassadors in residence across Europe. Using Fleury Vindry’s collections of ambassadors abroad, we can view the explosion of France’s diplomacy abroad.3 Arranging these embassies by total years present–for instance, two ambassadors, not including their retinues, present during one year, produce a value osf two for that year–we can visualize France’s diplomatic priorities and how they changed over time.

Figures 1 and 2 demonstrate the dramatic increase in France’s diplomatic presence abroad. For instance, between 1515 and 1525, France maintained only twenty-two total years of ambassadorial presence in six different states.


The significance of the Battle of Pavia (1525) to increasing France’s diplomatic outreach is dramatic. The battle was decisive. King Francis I himself was taken prisoner.4 The crushing defeat caused the French court to recognize the importance of a robust diplomatic network. Between 1525 and 1535, France maintained seventy-seven cumulative years of ambassadorial presence abroad in twelve separate states.


France’s diplomatic network only grew from there. In the twenty years before 1535, France did not have a single year during which ten separate ambassadors were dispatched. In the twenty years after 1535, there were fifteen. The axis on which this network centered extended from England to Switzerland, Venice, and the Ottoman Empire–the four states most densely populated with French diplomats.

In 1559, the conflict between France and the Habsburgs came to an end with the Treaty of Cateau-Cambrésis. Shortly thereafter, a series of religious civil wars broke out in 1562, dominating French concerns until they ended in 1629.5 Naturally, these wars have caused many historians to focus on France’s domestic concerns during the second half of the sixteenth century. Three diplomatic events have drawn historians’ attention during this period: various marriage negotiations between French princes and Queen Elizabeth of England; support extended to the Dutch as they revolted against Habsburg Spain; and the election of Henry of Valois to the Polish throne.6 Beyond this instance, French diplomacy during this period remains relatively unexplored. Nevertheless, as Figures 4-6 indicate, the French court maintained a robust diplomatic network in place. Moreover, the same diplomatic axis remained in place despite dramatically different international circumstances following 1559.
